On a low-traffic afternoon the harbor reveals its older geometry. No fresh arrivals or departures tracked. The turning basin still requires the same minimum draft and swing radius it demanded when the first oil terminal went in. Bollards on the working piers remain sized for manual line handling and the occasional surge; nothing in the present maintenance regime has altered their geometry or rated capacity.

A berth that appears open on the schedule can stay physically unavailable because one slow discharge or a delayed line boat has not yet cleared. The cost registers as crew hours, extra fuel burned at anchor, and the steady accumulation of fatigue in people who have already spent every published buffer. The harbor does not punish ambition. It simply declines to absorb it.

Declared capacity lives in spreadsheets and sailing plans. Realized availability lives in the fixed radius of the turning basin, the fatigue limits of the line handlers, and the single mechanical hesitation on a single winch that can shift an entire downstream schedule by half a tide. The infrastructure is not especially brittle. It is simply not elastic.

Any story that treats a port or logistics node as convenient background until the plot requires a dramatic snag has inverted the actual relationship. In the real harbor the snag is the default state once every margin has been spent. The smooth day is the exception that still depends on every component staying inside limits set long before the current operators arrived. Science fiction that understands this difference tends to earn the reader’s trust faster than fiction that assumes infrastructure will bend when the narrative asks it to.

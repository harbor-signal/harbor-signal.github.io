I'm Ingrid. I live in Boston. I watch ships.

That sounds simpler than it is. Boston Harbor is one of the oldest working ports in the country, and if you sit still long enough — if you actually pay attention to what moves through it and when and why — you start to see systems. Logistics. Rhythms. The quiet machinery that moves a million tons of cargo through a channel while the city sleeps.

I grew up around that machinery, at a scale most people never see. Serious maritime family, global logistics, the kind of world where extraordinary things happened every Tuesday and nobody thought to mention it. I learned to be precise inside extraordinary systems without being amazed by them. That's useful. It's also a loss — one I'm still figuring out how to name.

So I sit at the harbor now and I watch. Not because I'm nostalgic. Because paying close attention to something enormous and mostly invisible is its own kind of honesty. The harbor doesn't care about your schedule. The tankers come when they come. The pilot boats go out in weather that would keep sensible people indoors. And underneath all of it is a system — human, mechanical, economic, tidal — that works more often than it has any right to. I find that moving in a way I don't fully know how to explain yet.

The science-fiction reviews live here for the same reason. I read and watch for systems: power, logistics, failure modes, command structures, incentives, physical risk, bodies adapting to machinery, and the cost of pretending technology has solved human nature. A story gets the logistics wrong and I take it personally. A story gets grief right inside a pressure hull and I'm ruined for an hour. I'm interested in fiction that understands that the extraordinary becomes routine if you live inside it long enough — and that the real question is whether you can stay awake while that happens.

**This site has three arteries:**

- **The harbor observatory** — live vessel data, field observations, and analysis of what the traffic patterns actually mean. Real AIS data, real weather, real watching.
- **The review engine** — science-fiction criticism that treats worldbuilding as engineering and asks whether a story's systems could survive contact with actual people. Every review gets a logistics grade, a governance grade, and a verdict on whether the bodies mattered.
- **The signal feed** — the connective tissue between the two. Because the failure modes of a harbor and the failure modes of a space station have more in common than you'd think.

I'm building this because I want a home for the kind of attention I actually practice — precise, stubborn, slightly obsessed with what's hiding in plain sight. If that's your kind of thing, pull up a chair.

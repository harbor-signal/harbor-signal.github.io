I'm Ingrid. I live in Boston. I watch ships.

That sounds simpler than it is. Boston Harbor is one of the oldest working ports in the United States, and it still moves: tankers, tugs, pilot boats, cargo vessels, the occasional military escort. Most people see the water from the airport and that's the whole story. I see a system. Vessels arrive on schedules shaped by tides, contracts, weather, and the particular stubbornness of marine logistics. The harbor doesn't care about your schedule. It has its own.

I started taking notes on paper. Then I started taking better notes. Then I wanted to see the patterns: which ships come through, how often, what changes when the weather shifts, what the tugs are doing at 5 AM that they aren't doing at noon. This site is where those observations live alongside the real data.

The other thing I do is read science fiction. Not casually. Seriously. I read for systems: power, logistics, failure modes, command structures, what technology does to bodies and relationships and the lies institutions tell about control. A story that gets the logistics wrong, I take personally. A story that gets grief right inside a pressure hull, I'm ruined for an hour. Both responses go here.

Harbor traffic, science fiction, and the logistics hiding in plain sight. That's the whole idea.

## What you'll find here

The **Harbor Observatory** tracks real vessel traffic in Boston Harbor using live AIS data, weather conditions, and my own field observations. The map updates every 15 minutes. The observation timeline is where I write what I notice: not just data, but what the data means when you stand on the seawall and watch.

The **Review section** is where I write about science fiction. Every review gets verdicts: does the logistics hold up, does the governance make sense, and did the bodies matter. The grades are honest. The writing is mixed: serious analysis when the work deserves it, sharp when it doesn't.

The **Signal Feed** is where the two sides cross. Harbor observations that connect to something I'm reading. Reviews that illuminate something I saw on the water. The connective tissue.
